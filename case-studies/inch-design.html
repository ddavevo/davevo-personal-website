01 - Background

Inch-sized interfaces and pixel estate

One of the first design projects I worked on during COGS125 was creating UIs for various scales. Particularly, I was drawn to the challenge behind the inch-scale. There's not a lot of room to work with, so:


How do you ensure a 1 x 1 interface is easy to navigate?

Problem

How might users get enough daily vitamin D?


It's quite profound how we've transported so much of our day-to-day lives and routines on our devices. We do everything on a computer nowadays. And as a result, we're always cooped up inside and not being active.

User Persona

Meet June, working from home per usual.


June works long hours indoors and recently found out she has a vitamin D deficiency.

Her doctor explained that low vitamin D can affect her energy and overall health, so she’s trying to find ways to improve it.

But her routine rarely takes her outside, and she often forgets about it during the day.

How might we incorporate outdoor time into her indoor-heavy lifestyle?

Solution Preview

Easily see how much longer you need.

02 - The Initial Idea

Where the vitamin D tracker started

Initial Assumption

Starting out, I thought the key to this problem was encouraging consistency.


Wanting to be healthier is a common goal we all have. Maybe, we're motivated by a New Year's resolution for example.

My first design

I started off showing various representations of vitamin D needs, including:


  • Overall progress
  • Time remaining outside
  • Weekly performance

Getting User Feedback

"If all June needs to do is get enough sunlight each day, why is the time remaining the smallest thing on the screen?"


This was the question that caught me off guard during a design crit. That's a great question, you're right, the most important information should use the most pixels on the screen.


It was clear people didn’t care much about all these fancy data visualizations.
People don’t need to know how to do something, they just want to do it.

03 - Key Iterations

How the vitamin D tracker evolved

Getting straight to the point

My revised design was entirely focused on tracking on a day-to-day basis, with an emphasis on how much time left they need to be outside.

✅

Easiest way to assess progress

Instead of using the scientific unit IU, it was easier to say how much longer they still needed.

✅

Removed unnecessary info

Removed the weekly performance graph to better prioritize daily goal-setting.

✅

Ensuring user flexibility

Added a secondary button for supplement tracking if users meet their vitamin D goals differently.


Taking it a step further

I took it a step further to improve accessibility for a wider range of users. My professor shared his insights on agnostic designs that work across languages and cultures, and I wanted to challenge myself on achieving something that's universally understood.

✅

Universal iconography

Instead of using text to represent secondary info such as "time remaining", I used a sun icon.

✅

Precision isn't always necessary

I simplified the rounding to show 1.5 hours instead of 1 hr, 26 minutes.

The suggestion was odd initially, but does it really matter if the user sees 1 hr, 26 minutes compared to 1 hr, 30 minutes?

03 - Reflection

And that's a wrap...

I have a stronger appreciation for designers who work on watch UIs. Especially the pioneers of the Apple Watch, they're geniuses. Inspired by this project, I looked up their demos and saw how seamless and thought out each interaction is, it's incredible. I have a long ways to go.

And what did I learn?

Bridging interactions and semantics

When you have less pixels to work with, everything has to be goal-oriented for the user. With each iteration, I gained insight on how to communicate more information with less space. Using icons instead of words, making shapes take on more meaning, framing everything around the words that are on the screen, and so on.

Dare to dream

Planning the UI's skeleton is crucial for ensuring the right things pop out. But it can limiting if you rely too much on existing UI patterns. What seems obvious does not always mean it's necessary. With this project, I was forced to unlearn these safety nets and be more willing to try different design ideas, even if it may not land. But there's always to chance that it will.
