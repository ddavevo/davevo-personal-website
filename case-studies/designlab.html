01 - Background

AI tools are major disrupters

The continuous rollout of novel AI-integrated tools has expedited the problem-solving process for many different fields. These tools aren’t just improving productivity.

They’re changing how people are approaching creativity altogether!

As these features are changing the game…

Many big questions continue to loom in the bigger picture.

To make the most out of these tools…

We need more concrete answers on how the human-AI dynamic impacts the process and end results.

How we explored our research question

How does the positioning of Al within a creative workflow impact creative outcomes, the allocation of cognitive resources, and user perceptions of agency and overall value of AI?

To explore an alternative approach for interacting with AI tools, we looked at integrating an LLM into design templates, a well-established strategy for helping users think, organize information, and reflect on creative work.

02 - Solution

How our plugin works

POV: Do you find brainstorming a potential solution to a problem challenging? Imagine where generative AI could support you in your workflow.

Engage with different perspectives by generating reflective questions.

Consolidate your line of reasoning by generating a root cause to the problem.

Gather unique insights from competing solutions with a click of a button.

Discover new aspects of your idea and compare it with existing solutions.

03 - Research Methods

Research Methods

We ran a between-subjects experiment that randomly assigned N=47 users to one of the following three conditions:

No-AI (control)

Users approach a problem/solution without LLM assistance, manually filling out the templates based only on their current knowledge.

Co-Led

Users gain access to LLM generation features in specific parts of the templates, assisting with reflection or proposing alternative ideas.

AI-Led

Templates are already filled out by AI. Users don't have to initiate any writing, only reading and processing what was generated.

The research team and I led user studies that had participants walk through the problem solving process using the template plugin, followed by surveys and structured interviews.

How we measured user outcomes across conditions

Something I learned in the process of defining the methodology is ensuring we’re not analyzing the tool’s performance, but rather the effects the tool has on the users.

At its core, we were curious how the plugin shaped their thinking and creative outcomes, using literature-backed metrics and frameworks that assess reflective thinking, creativity, and usability.

04 - Results

Results & Takeaways

No-AI participants generated fewer topic categories but did the most self-revision, spending more time refining half-baked ideas. They had a strong understanding of the problem, rated their solutions as more creative, and expressed higher confidence by thinking deeply without outside help.

Co-Led participants rated their initial solutions as creative, drawing new perspectives through guided reflection rather than relying on AI content. While they explored more topics than No-AI, they rated their iterated solutions as less competitive in the market, focusing more on realistic constraints than novelty.

AI-Led participants covered the most topic categories in their iterated solutions, given immediate access to AI-generated information. However, they struggled to form new perspectives, made the fewest edits, and required more time to narrow down their solution’s scope, resulting in lower creativity ratings.

What does this mean?

Problem-solving is a swinging pendulum between gathering information, revising earlier ideas, and creating new ones.

While No-AI participants engaged deeply with the problem,

They were burdened with keeping track of everything as their understanding evolved or gathering more context, which can leave less time for actually synthesizing ideas together.

While AI-led participants had exposure to more perspectives and ideas early on,

Users found it hard to explore beyond the AI’s suggestions because they seemed complete and convincing. As a result, they needed much more time to digest the templates before making a decision on their idea’s direction.

And due to the AI’s perceived comprehensiveness, some users even accepted surface-level ideas without fully questioning them.

Co-Led participants struck more of a happy medium

AI helped expand their thinking while staying active in shaping/challenging ideas without being overwhelmed by information.

However, having a more complex understanding made them less confident in their idea’s success, as they grappled with unanswered “what-ifs” they weren’t sure how to resolve easily.

So then, how should products provide AI support?

Proactively help users drill deeper and expand their perspectives

Match support to what users need the most

Ensure users feel confident and in control while navigating complexity

05 - Our Journey

Research Process Learnings

Our research process began with coding timestamps for key task activities and running thematic analyses on user interviews responses to pinpoint patterns.

However, we initially struggled to build a cohesive, data-driven narrative—partly because we were treating qualitative and quantitative data as separate silos.

Data isn’t just words and numbers

The turning point came when we reframed them not as “words” and “numbers,” but as reflections of “what participants thought” and “what they did.”

And so, we reversed our approach

Rather than beginning with thought processes and searching for matching behaviors, we started with statistically significant behaviors and traced them back to supporting quotes.

This clarified the why behind users’ actions and gave us a clearer lens to explain the various effects on their thinking.

An exploration that didn’t work out

In a pre-survey, we asked users if they had any design thinking background. Then, experts blindly rated user responses across scaling benchmarks, which we graphed against experience levels.

We were surprised to see that there’s no strong correlation. But that’s when we realized something:

Solution quality is not black and white – what qualities define a strong solution?

When you take the users’ solutions away from where the learning took place, others can’t see why certain choices were made.

Some may associate higher word counts with more developed ideas, but others may see wordy responses as fluff. Or some may find a solution with multiple ideas as multi-faceted while others may think it feels scattered and lacks direction.

Our most meaningful findings were about how users learned and felt during the process—not just what they produced.

That’s why our final thesis argues that AI tools should go beyond generating outputs and instead help users reflect, trace their thinking, and make their process more seamless. That’s the real value.

06 - Reflection

Contributing to HCI in the era of AI

Thank you UCSD Design Lab for bringing me on! I valued the opportunity to contribute to the growth of creativity support tools (CSTs) and take initiative in various aspects of the research process.

Learning | Humanizing our key findings

I thought research was only about pushing the envelope and finding novelty. Over these 10 months, I realized that results only matter if they reflect something about us, the people who use and get influenced by this type of technology. Data-driven storytelling is just as important as knowing how to wrangle information together.

Challenge | Doing academic research for the first time

It’s no secret that academic research demands rigor and many hats to be worn. While it took time to get up to speed with the literature and methods, the biggest lesson was staying present, being a self-starter, and staying hungry for more.

What I would have done differently | Getting more perspectives from other researchers early on

I navigated the highs and lows of HCI research using the limited methods I knew at the time. While we got there in the end, I’ve since realized how valuable it would be to actively seek mentorship from more academics.
