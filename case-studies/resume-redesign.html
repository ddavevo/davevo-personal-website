01 - Background

What makes a strong resume?

Recruiters spend an average of 6 seconds skimming a resume. In that 6 seconds, they assess a candidate's skills and determine if they're qualified enough to follow up with.

Problem

But then, why do my applications keep getting rejected?


While there may be many factors that contributes to this issue, the bottom line is likely this: my resume doesn't stand out, but more importantly, the page isn't grabbing their attention.

How might I guide recruiters' eyes to the most memorable points on my resume?

Solution Preview

Removing unnecessary ink

The most prominent information should use the most ink, so whenever possible, I simplified the wording, removed unnecessary information, and made headers smaller and more compact.

Bold and bigger

Whenever I bold phrases, I'll bump the font size up a few points to make them more noticable from the rest of the resume bullets.

02 - The Initial Idea

The latest version of my resume

To figure out how I might improve my resume, I decided to start with my current version and asked what I liked and what I wished was better.

Initial Assumption

Starting at rejection

I asked myself "Why did my resume get rejected?"


My best guess has to do with the content itself. Maybe there weren't enough numbers? Did the content not align well enough with the role?

However...

It's not necessarily a content issue, it's a visual one

Although the Experience section is the main seller, what about everything else on the page?


  • Top Ribbon

If Experience is most important, the name shouldn't be the biggest element on the page.


  • Education

If you're applying to industry jobs, there's no reason to highlight classwork that's you're already taking in your major. Also, people already expect Education to include degree type, expected graduation date, and GPA.


  • Headers

The headers for Skills, Experience, and Education are bigger than the content. But people are already expecting these sections anyways.

03 - Reflection

And that's a wrap...

It was interesting applying a design lens on a seemingly ordinary part of the job hunting process. Instead of treating each resume section as an essential part of the equation, we assessed each one's importance the ground up and reimagined how it could be better optimized for its role.

And what did I learn?

Don't take anything for granted

This assignment provided me a moment to look back on when I first designed my resume and why I added certain pieces of content. I realize now that visual hierarchy isn't just make some text bigger or smaller, it's whether text should even be there in the first place as well.

What does it mean to read a resume?

Even though I've been conditioned to track my accomplishments for each role, I've always assumed that's all you need for the resume to work. But going back to my original question, "Why does my resume keep getting rejected?", it's clear that there's more to the story if the resume isn't doing what it's supposed to. So I'll be remembering this for my future job hunting.
